Aortic Stenosis

What is it?

Narrowing of the valve in the large blood vessel branching off the heart. This narrowing keeps the valve from opening fully, reducing blood flow to the body and making the heart work harder.

Causes of it

  • High blood pressure
  • Abnormal lipids
  • Diabetes
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Some individuals are felt to be genetically predisposed to aortic stenosis

Symptoms of it

  • Chest discomfort: The chest pain may get worse with activity and reach into the arm, neck, or jaw.
  • Cough, possibly bloody.
  • Breathing problems when exercising.
  • Becoming easily tired.
  • Feeling the heartbeat (palpitations).
  • Fainting, weakness, or dizziness with activity.

How to lower the risk

  • Eating a healthy diet with less salt and more potassium-rich foods.
  • Losing weight, if needed.
  • Finding ways to eliminate stress.

Treatment for it

You can treat aortic stenosis by using both surgical and nonsurgical procedures including: Aortic valve replacement surgery, where cardiac surgeons remove the damaged valve through open-chest or minimally invasive heart surgery and replace it with a mechanical or biological valve (made from cow, pig or human heart tissue).
